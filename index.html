Asking Good Questions?

Wednesday, January 7th, 2015

How do you ask a good question? Why, that’s a good question… Asking good questions is a very important part of the learning process. This is especially true in DBC. So much of the “Kitchen” philosophy depends on asking questions and taking responsibility for your own education (If unfamiliar, read blog below on Kitchen V. Table). We all grew up being told that there is no such thing as a dumb question, that may not be necessarily true. Making sure you ask the question correctly will help ensure that you will get the answer you’re looking for.

First off read your question. Seems obvious right? If you read your question and don’t understand it; then how would some else? Im sure everyone has done this before. Sometimes it takes a lot to finally ask a question. By the time you do, your mind is so wrapped up in the problem that you ask a question that has no context. Context is so important when you ask a question, you have to paint the picture for the person to fully grasp the situation. Set the stage! Explain everything that is relevant to answering the question: Program Language, Version, OS etc..

How should I ask good question. Notice I didn’t use a ‘?’… Make sure that you are actually asking a question. I am guilty of this. I start explaining the situation and say “I did this and it broke..”. So, yes it broke. What did you expect would happened? We’ll obviously not break, but when asking a question you need to do better then that. Get more factual and give an expectation, then ask “Why didn’t that meet expectations?”. If there are error messages throw that in with setting the stage, giving all possible information and expectations will help you get the answer you are looking for.

And finally, make a good impression. I know this may not seem like it has anything to do with actually asking the question. Which it doesn’t, however it has everything to do with you getting the answer you’re looking for. Making a good impression will help you get the audience you’re looking for. If your question looks terrible (i.e. Spelling, grammar, punctuation) then you might get a terrible answer. This is good practice for life. You have to play to your audience. If Einstein was scrolling down the page and saw your question and the presentation was sloppy and/or poorly worded; would he stop to read it? Maybe, but probably not. You would drastically improve your chances of someone with sufficient grey matter to answer your question (the way your looking for) if was clear and presented properly.

NoSQL

Thursday, January 3rd, 2015

Welcome back to my blog! During this installment we're going to be discussing NoSQL databases (DBs). Here is a very helpful that help me understand what NoSQL is. By looking at the name you would think that NoSQL is as it sounds, but it's actually "Not only SQL". This is another type of database management system.

So why would I not use a relational database?
One instance would be when you have a massive amount of data and want to maintain a level of performance. When using a relational DB it can get messy with many, many to many relationships. That doesn't bode well for performance, joining tables and making complex queries could really bog you down and slow the DB down. Also, what if the data is not structured? (Like this blog) You would probably go for a NoSQL type DB.

Sounds great right? So when wouldn't I want to use one?
Well, whenever you need the DB to preform complex transactions would be one instance. Anytime you would be putting more pressure on the DB to preform tasks rather then just storing is probably when you would think about not using a NoSQL DB.

In conclusion, if you need the DB to provide constraints and/or preform complex transactions you probably would not choose a NoSQL type data structure. However, if your primary focus is horizontal scalability and fast performance, then NoSQL is probably what you're looking for. I highly suggest you watch the video link above. It offers great insight into this subject. Thanks for reading!!

The Stereotype Threat

Friday, December 5th, 2014

Welcome back to my blog. I'm pretty sure no one is reading it by now, but if you are thanks. This installment is on the stereotype threat. There were a few sources I used for research on this topic and by far the best one was a lecture by Claude Steele at Columbia University. Links will be posed below. The results of some of his experiments really blew my mind so if you have time I really recommend that video. Anyway, the stereo type threat. Basically it's the threat of being reduced to a stereotype.

Imagine this... You are an awesome math student, and you have a math test coming up. The only problem is that you are a woman, and you were told that women do terrible on this test. So you take it and you do terrible. Now you already had pre-affirmed reason why you did terrible. Effectively placing the blame on the identity rather then the individual. It makes it very easy to justify why you did so terrible. What before the test they told you that women typically get the same scores as men? As you probably guessed, the women do significantly better.

The power of suggestion seems paramount here. If you think about it, society, our whole life has been suggestive. It's all about what social group you identify with. When put in a stressful or difficult situation it's natural for us to identify with whatever social group we feel fit the situation contextually. Claude Steele mad several awesome points pertaining to this type of situation. So seeing suggestive things all around our society is also shaping individuals to act like the identity.

It seems to work both ways. If the relevant group that you identify with contextually does poorly in said situation then the individual will more than likely underperform and meet standard set by the identity. If you take away the threat of being a stereotype, also not having your identity to blame if you come up short, you get more individual based results.

I know this is a lot easier said than done, but I think of Rene' Descartes... "I think, therefore I am(or exist)". I think this a good way to think about it. Rather than identifying with a social group try to channel yourself in stressful situations. Not having an excuse or a reason you acted a certain way. It's a new day in age and it's terrible to fall back on old stereotypes. Hopefully this good was food for thought. Check this video out!!

Feedback

Sunday, November 30th, 2014

Hey! Welcome back to my blog, I appreciate you taking the time to read it. This post is going to be about feedback. During the DBC experience feedback becomes a way of life. Giving and receiving feedback is an intricate part of the learning experience. So much of what you do as a developer is going to be under the microscope so you better be prepared to receive positive and negative feedback.

I find that writing feedback that is kind, actionable, and specific; is a lot harder then it sounds. Because these people are my peers and I will be getting extremely close to them over the next few months. I found it difficult to say negative things about them initially. This is all apart of the process. How will you know you are making a mistake if no one tells you? That's why DBC uses the A.S.K. (Actionable, Kind, and Specific) acronym. Initially I thought feedback was a negative thing but it's really all about feedback. It's were all the growth happens. By being A.S.K. your not putting anyone down, you're helping them grow as a programmer.

Over all I think the pairing aspect of DBC is where I learn the most. Because there are two views and perspectives of the challenge, and in ruby there are a plethora of ways to solve the problem, this is where the learning happens. Bouncing ideas off of each other, talking the problem out, trying and failing then overcoming adversity together. My peer programing experience has been overwhelmingly good, also to my surprise.

When receiving feedback you have to take it for what it's worth. Put it in perspective and if it's negative, learn from it. If you don't agree with it then take it with a grain of salt but try and remain objective. Not everyone is their best everyday. So look at your self honestly... It's helps to have another set of eyes look at you while you work. Like I said previously, it's all about growth and without feedback there will be no growth.

Margin, Border, Padding: What's the difference?

Thursday, November 7th, 2014

The diagram below, I feel, gives a great visual representation of the differences between the three. But, let's start by explaining that all three are the blueprint of the object/element they describe. Giving values to these will do several things, but mainly, they will allow you to possition your element where ever you want it to be on the page and manipulate the content inside.

Let's start with Margin. This is your main attribute that allows you to place your element on the page. Giving it a value of let's say, (margin-left: 50px;). That will move your object away from the left side of the screen by 50 pixels. Same can be said by all; left/right/top/bottom. If you give it a value of auto; (margin: auto;) It will give all attributes equal value.. Centering it in the page, in-between elements of course. Get it? Don't worry if you don't yet, once you start playing around with it, it starts making a lot more sense.. Now let's move on to border...

Border is the space in between the margin and padding. Let's say you wanna make a button, you want to give it definition so it stands out from the rest of the page or container it's in. So a typical value for a border looks like this; (border: 2px solid black;). First giving it a size of 2 pixels, it can be whatever you'd like. The type of border is solid, can also be dashed and dotted(im sure there's more). And finally giving it a color of black, also can be any color you'd like. Adding a border is an awesome way of giving definition to your element, making it stand out from other thing on your page... And then there's the padding.

Padding is the space between the actual content of your element and the border. Let's say you have a paragraph in your element and it's right up against your left border. You want to move it to the right without moving your border.. Then it's time to put some padding between them!.. So, the padding command would generally look the same as the margin command; (padding-left: 50px;). That would give you a 50 pixel padding between your border and the content in your element. Also, instead of using px(pixels), it's good to use percentages(10%). Just so it will adjust to the browser, 50px on my computer might not look like 50px on yours!
